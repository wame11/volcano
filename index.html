Natural causes of climate change

Volcanoes can briefly cool the planet.

Huge eruptions blast ash and sulfur dioxide high into the atmosphere. That sulfur can become tiny reflective particles, blocking some sunlight and causing short-term global cooling.

1. What is it?

Volcanic activity is Earth releasing pressure.

Volcanoes form where magma rises from inside Earth and escapes through cracks, vents, or craters. Eruptions can release lava, ash, rocks, steam, carbon dioxide, and sulfur gases.

They are natural climate influences, but they do not explain modern long-term warming. Their biggest climate effect is usually temporary cooling after very explosive eruptions.

2. How does it work?

Pressure builds, then the volcano vents.

  1. 1 Magma rises because it is hot and less dense than solid rock.
  2. 2 Gas bubbles expand as pressure drops near the surface.
  3. 3 If pressure wins, ash, lava, and gases burst out.
  4. 4 Powerful eruptions can reach the stratosphere, where particles last longer.

3. How does it affect climate?

Sulfur aerosols act like a temporary sunshade.

Sulfur dioxide from major eruptions reacts in the atmosphere to form sulfate aerosols. These tiny droplets reflect sunlight back to space, so Earth's surface can cool for months to a few years.

4. Evidence and examples

The 1991 Mount Pinatubo eruption is the clearest example.

Satellites tracked a global aerosol cloud after Pinatubo erupted in the Philippines. Scientists measured less incoming sunlight and a short dip in global temperatures, matching the idea that volcanic aerosols cool the planet temporarily.

5. Importance

Volcanoes matter, but they are not the main cause of today's warming.

Volcanic eruptions help scientists understand natural climate swings. They can cool Earth briefly, damage air quality, and affect flights and farming. However, their effect is short-lived compared with the long-term warming caused by human greenhouse gas emissions.

Quick takeaway:Volcanoes can change climate fast, but only for a short time.
